Eisenstein Geometry and the Dark Dispersion Relation: a Cross-Term Interpretation of the Sirag Factor

Lee McCulloch-James
Draft, 2026
Abstract

The Sirag large-number coincidence equates $2^{1/\alpha}$, where $\alpha \approx 1/137$ is the fine-structure constant, with the inverse gravitational coupling $\hbar c / G m_p m_e$, up to a residual factor of $\approx 1.74$. Rather than absorbing this discrepancy into a varying gravitational constant, we interpret it as a structural analogy with the cross-term that appears when an orthogonal quadratic form is replaced by the hexagonal (Eisenstein) norm $a^2 + ab + b^2$. Applied to the energy-momentum relation, this cross-term takes the form $mc|\vec{p}|c^2$, the geometric mean of the rest-mass energy squared and the radiation momentum energy squared. We propose this as a kinematic ansatz for dark matter specifically. The connection to the $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3) suggests a link between hexagonal gauge geometry and the GeV mass scale inferred from the Sirag relation via a cosmological geometric mean construction.

Notation and conventions

$\alpha = e^2 / 4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar c \approx 1/137.036$: fine-structure constant (low-energy, Thomson limit)

$m_e, m_p, m_n$: electron, proton, neutron masses

$G, \hbar, c$: gravitational constant, reduced Planck constant, speed of light

$m_{\rm Pl} = \sqrt{\hbar c / G}$: Planck mass

$\Omega_b, \Omega_c$: baryon and cold dark matter density parameters

$f = \Omega_c / (\Omega_c + \Omega_b) \approx 0.84$: dark fraction of total matter

$p = |\vec{p}\,|$: magnitude of 3-momentum (scalar, non-negative)

$X = pc,\; Y = mc^2$: positive scalar energy-dimensioned variables

1The standard relativistic quadratic form

The relativistic dispersion relation follows from the Minkowski norm of the four-momentum. For a particle with four-momentum $P^\mu = (E/c,\, \vec{p}\,)$, the invariant mass condition is

$\displaystyle P_\mu P^\mu = m^2 c^2,$ (1)

which gives

$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4,$ (2)

where $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ throughout this paper. Setting $X = pc$ and $Y = mc^2$, both positive scalars with dimensions of energy, this becomes

$\displaystyle E^2 = X^2 + Y^2.$ (3)

This is the Euclidean norm: the orthogonal quadratic form on two variables. The corresponding lattice is the square lattice $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ of the Gaussian integers. The orthogonality of $X$ and $Y$ reflects the Lorentz invariance of the dispersion relation: momentum and rest-mass energy contribute independently, with no coupling between them.

2The Eisenstein hexagonal form

If the underlying geometry is hexagonal, with basis vectors at $60°$, the norm on the lattice $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ of Eisenstein integers ($\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$) becomes

Eisenstein norm
$\displaystyle \|z\|^2 = X^2 + XY + Y^2.$ (4)

Substituting $X = pc$ and $Y = mc^2$ gives

Modified dispersion relation
$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4 + mc^2 \cdot pc.$ (5)

The third term is the cross-term introduced by the $60°$ lattice geometry. Note that because $p = |\vec{p}\,| \geq 0$ and $m \geq 0$, the cross-term is non-negative and well-defined. We treat equation (5) as a kinematic ansatz on the two positive scalar variables $X$ and $Y$. We are not claiming Lorentz covariance for this expression.

3The cross-term as geometric mean

The two diagonal terms in the dispersion relation are $Y^2 = (mc^2)^2$ and $X^2 = (pc)^2$. The first is the square of the inertial rest-mass energy. The second is the square of the radiation-like momentum energy. Their geometric mean is

$\displaystyle \sqrt{Y^2 \cdot X^2} = XY = mc^2 \cdot pc.$ (6)
Geometric mean structure

The Eisenstein cross-term is exactly the geometric mean of the two quantities it interpolates: rest-mass energy squared and radiation momentum energy squared. It sits between the massive ($p = 0$) and massless ($m = 0$) limits, coupling the two regimes. In any quadratic form $X^2 + \lambda XY + Y^2$, the cross-term $XY$ is the geometric mean of $X^2$ and $Y^2$. The Eisenstein geometry selects $\lambda = 1$.

4The Sirag coincidence and its discrepancy

Let $\alpha$ denote the fine-structure constant,

$\displaystyle \alpha = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar c} \approx \frac{1}{137.036}.$ (7)

We use the low-energy (Thomson limit) value throughout. Sirag [1] observed that two dimensionless quantities are numerically close:

$\displaystyle N_\alpha = 2^{1/\alpha} \approx 1.79 \times 10^{41}, \qquad N_G = \frac{\hbar c}{G\, m_p\, m_e} = \frac{m_{\rm Pl}^2}{m_p m_e} \approx 3.11 \times 10^{41}.$ (8)

Their ratio is

Sirag factor
$\displaystyle \frac{N_G}{N_\alpha} \approx 1.74.$ (9)

Taking logarithms,

$\displaystyle \ln N_G = \ln N_\alpha + \ln 1.74, \qquad \ln 1.74 \approx 0.554.$ (10)

The discrepancy is an additive contribution of $\approx 0.554$ in the logarithm of the large number.

5The discrepancy as structural analogue of the cross-term

The connection between the Sirag factor and the Eisenstein cross-term is a structural analogy, not a derivation. We state it precisely.

In the orthogonal norm $E^2 = X^2 + Y^2$, the two contributions are independent. In the Eisenstein norm $E^2 = X^2 + XY + Y^2$, the cross-term $XY$ increases the total by the factor

$\displaystyle \frac{X^2 + XY + Y^2}{X^2 + Y^2} = 1 + \frac{XY}{X^2 + Y^2}.$ (11)

For two positive quantities $X$ and $Y$ with $X \neq Y$, this ratio lies strictly between 1 and 1.5 (the maximum occurring at $X = Y$). The Sirag factor of 1.74 exceeds this range, so the identification is not exact at the level of the norm ratio.

However, the analogy operates at the level of structure rather than numerical identity: the Sirag discrepancy is an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ multiplicative correction to a large-number coincidence, and the Eisenstein cross-term is an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ multiplicative correction to a quadratic norm. Both arise from departing from orthogonality. The proposal is that these two departures share a common geometric origin.

More concretely: if we impose the exact relation $2^{1/\alpha} = m_{\rm Pl}^2 / (m_e m_X)$ and solve for $m_X$, we obtain $m_X / m_p = N_G / N_\alpha \approx 1.74$. The question is then what physics produces this effective mass. The Eisenstein geometry, through the cross-term, provides a candidate mechanism.

6The dark dispersion relation

We propose that the kinematic ansatz

Dark sector dispersion
$\displaystyle E^2 = X^2 + XY + Y^2 = p^2 c^2 + mc^2 \!\cdot\! pc + m^2 c^4$ (12)

applies specifically to dark matter, and not to baryonic matter.

The cross-term $XY = mc^2 \cdot pc$ couples rest-mass energy and momentum energy. At rest ($p = 0$, so $X = 0$) the relation reduces to $E = mc^2$. For massless particles ($m = 0$, so $Y = 0$) it reduces to $E = pc$. The modification appears only when both mass and momentum are nonzero: that is, for massive particles in motion.

For baryonic matter, the orthogonal dispersion relation $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m^2c^4$ has been verified to extraordinary precision across particle physics. Any cross-term for baryonic matter is excluded. The claim is therefore sector-specific: the Eisenstein cross-term modifies the kinematics of massive dark particles in motion, while leaving baryonic kinematics and photon propagation untouched.

The cross-term, as shown in §3, is the geometric mean of the rest-mass energy squared and the momentum energy squared. It interpolates between the two regimes that baryonic physics treats as orthogonal. For dark matter, these regimes are not orthogonal but coupled at $60°$.

7Connection with the $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3)

The root lattice of SU(3) is the hexagonal $A_2$ lattice. The Cartan matrix of SU(3) is

$\displaystyle A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$ (13)

The corresponding quadratic form on the root space is

$\displaystyle \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{x}^T A\,\mathbf{x} = a^2 - ab + b^2.$ (14)

Under the reflection $b \to -b$ this becomes $a^2 + ab + b^2$, the Eisenstein norm. The lattice generated is unchanged by this reflection: both forms represent the same $A_2$ hexagonal lattice up to basis choice.

QCD, with gauge group SU(3)$_c$, generates the dominant contribution to the proton mass via confinement at scale $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200$ MeV. A dark confining gauge sector with SU(3)-like structure would naturally inherit the hexagonal geometry of the $A_2$ root lattice.

Key observation

The Eisenstein cross-term in the dispersion relation and the hexagonal root lattice of SU(3) share the same mathematical structure. The cross-term is not imposed ad hoc. It is the natural kinematic form associated with a gauge symmetry whose root system is hexagonal.

8On Poincaré invariance

The cross-term $XY = mc^2 \cdot pc$ is not a Lorentz scalar. Under a boost, $m$ is invariant but $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ is not, so $XY$ is not boost-invariant. The kinematic ansatz (12) therefore breaks Poincaré symmetry.

This is a strong claim. Poincaré invariance is foundational to relativistic quantum field theory. The CPT theorem depends on it. No violation has been observed in any sector, baryonic or otherwise.

On the status of Poincaré invariance in the dark sector

We do not argue that Poincaré invariance is wrong. We note that it has been tested exclusively with electromagnetically coupled matter. The CPT theorem applies to quantum fields on a Poincaré-invariant background. Whether dark matter is described by such fields is an assumption, not a measurement.

The ansatz (12) is not directly constrained by laboratory Lorentz tests, which probe baryonic and leptonic sectors. It is, however, constrained indirectly by cosmological observables: structure formation, CMB anisotropy, and limits on dark matter self-interaction and free-streaming all depend on dark matter kinematics. A full confrontation with cosmological data is beyond the scope of this heuristic note, but would be required to establish or exclude the ansatz observationally.

We note that the ansatz vanishes for massless particles, vanishes at rest, and preserves spatial isotropy (since $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ is rotation-invariant). It breaks only boost invariance, and only for massive dark particles in motion.

9The GeV mass scale from geometric mean construction

The Sirag factor of 1.74 can be absorbed by defining an effective gravitating mass scale $m_X \approx 1.74\, m_p \approx 1.6$ GeV$/c^2$. This is the mass that makes the relation $2^{1/\alpha} = m_{\rm Pl}^2 / (m_e m_X)$ exact.

Decomposing $m_X$ into baryonic and dark contributions via the mass-fraction weighted geometric mean

$\displaystyle m_X = m_p^{\,1-f}\; m_{\rm dm}^{\,f}, \qquad f \approx 0.84,$ (15)

and solving for $m_{\rm dm}$ yields

Inferred dark matter mass
$\displaystyle m_{\rm dm} = m_p \left(\frac{m_X}{m_p}\right)^{1/f} \approx 2\;\text{GeV}/c^2.$ (16)

This is the same GeV scale generated by QCD confinement. Under the present interpretation, it arises because the hexagonal cross-term and the confining gauge dynamics share a common geometric origin in the $A_2$ root lattice.

The justification for using $m_p$ as the baryonic mass scale (rather than an average over all baryon and lepton species) is given in Appendix A.

10Discussion

The chain of reasoning is as follows.

(i) The Sirag factor $N_G / N_\alpha \approx 1.74$ is an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ multiplicative correction to a large-number coincidence. The Eisenstein cross-term is an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ multiplicative correction to a quadratic norm. We propose these share a common geometric origin, while acknowledging this is a structural analogy rather than a derivation.

(ii) The hexagonal quadratic form $X^2 + XY + Y^2$ is the Eisenstein norm, native to the $A_2$ root lattice. The cross-term $XY$ is the geometric mean of the rest-mass energy squared and the momentum energy squared.

(iii) The $A_2$ lattice is the root lattice of SU(3). QCD, with gauge group SU(3)$_c$, generates the proton mass at the GeV scale via confinement. A dark confining sector with SU(3)-like structure would produce dark matter masses at a comparable scale.

(iv) The geometric mean construction, using the cosmological dark fraction $f \approx 0.84$, yields $m_{\rm dm} \approx 2$ GeV, consistent with a dark confinement scale comparable to $\Lambda_{\rm QCD}$.

(v) The kinematic ansatz breaks Poincaré symmetry. This is a strong claim. It is not directly constrained by existing laboratory tests (which probe only electromagnetically coupled matter), but it is indirectly constrained by cosmological observables. A confrontation with structure formation data is required and is beyond the scope of this note.

Summary

The Sirag factor is structurally analogous to the Eisenstein cross-term. The Eisenstein geometry is the root geometry of SU(3). The mass scale it produces is the confinement scale. The cross-term is the geometric mean of the rest-mass and momentum contributions to the dispersion relation. These four observations are not independent.

ACosmological abundance justification

The choice of $m_p m_e$ as the mass product in the gravitational coupling $\alpha_g = G m_p m_e / \hbar c$ requires justification. One might ask why not average over all baryon and lepton species. The answer is that cosmological abundances select specific species overwhelmingly.

A.1   Baryon abundances

At the current CMB temperature $T_{\rm CMB} \approx 2.7$ K $\approx 2.3 \times 10^{-4}$ eV, any baryon heavier than the nucleon is suppressed by the Boltzmann factor $\exp(-\Delta mc^2 / kT)$. For the lightest strange baryon ($\Lambda$, mass gap $\Delta mc^2 \approx 177$ MeV):

$\displaystyle \exp\!\left(-\frac{177\;\text{MeV}}{2.3\times10^{-4}\;\text{eV}}\right) \approx \exp(-7.7\times10^{5}) \approx 0.$ (A.1)

The cosmic baryon population consists entirely of nucleons. Primordial helium synthesis fixes the mixture at approximately 87.5% protons and 12.5% neutrons ($n_p : n_n \approx 7 : 1$).

The number-weighted geometric mean nucleon mass is $m_N = m_p^{0.875}\, m_n^{0.125}$. Since $m_n / m_p \approx 1.00138$, this gives $m_N / m_p \approx 1.00017$, a correction at the level of $\mathcal{O}(10^{-4})$, negligible for the large-number relations.

A.2   Charged lepton abundances

The heavier charged leptons decay rapidly: $\tau_\mu \approx 2.2\;\mu$s, $\tau_\tau \approx 2.9 \times 10^{-13}$ s. Only electrons survive on cosmological timescales. Charge neutrality requires $n_e = n_p$.

For completeness: the arithmetic and geometric means of the three charged lepton masses $(m_e, m_\mu, m_\tau)$ with equal weights are approximately 628 MeV and 46 MeV respectively. Both are far too heavy to produce the correct order of magnitude in the gravitational coupling. The electron appears not because it is an "average lepton" but because it is the only cosmologically persistent one.

A.3   Neutrino abundances

The cosmic neutrino background contains approximately 112 neutrinos per cm$^3$ per flavour ($\nu + \bar{\nu}$), giving roughly 336 total [3]. These outnumber baryons by a factor of order $10^9$. However, neutrino masses are of order 10 to 50 meV, so the neutrino contribution to any gravitational coupling of the form $Gm_1 m_2 / \hbar c$ is negligible compared to the nucleon-electron product.

A number-weighted lepton average would be dominated by neutrino masses and would produce a gravitational coupling many orders of magnitude weaker than the Sirag target of $\sim 10^{-41}$. A mass-weighted average would be dominated by the tau and would overshoot. Neither matches. The $m_p m_e$ product is selected by cosmological persistence, not by convention.

A.4   Number densities and nucleon count

Present-day number densities (order of magnitude)

Baryons: $n_b \sim 2.5 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$ (of which $\sim$87.5% protons, $\sim$12.5% neutrons) [3]

Electrons: $n_e \sim 2.2 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$ (charge neutrality with protons)

Neutrinos: $n_\nu \sim 336$ cm$^{-3}$ (all flavours, $\nu + \bar{\nu}$) [3]

Neutrino-to-baryon ratio: $\sim 1.3 \times 10^{9}$

The number of baryons in the observable universe is $N_{\rm baryons} \sim 10^{80}$, giving $\sqrt{N} \sim 10^{40}$: the original Dirac large-number coincidence. Including dark matter ($\Omega_m \approx 0.315$) gives $N_{\rm total} \sim 6 \times 10^{80}$ in proton-mass equivalents, so $\sqrt{N_{\rm total}} \sim 2.5 \times 10^{40}$. The traditional $\sim 10^{40}$ holds well for baryonic matter alone.
