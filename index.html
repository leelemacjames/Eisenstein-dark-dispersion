Eisenstein Geometry and the Dark Mass-Generating Mechanism: a Cross-Term Interpretation of the Sirag Factor

Lee McCulloch-James
Draft, 2026
Abstract

The Sirag large-number coincidence equates $2^{1/\alpha}$, where $\alpha \approx 1/137$ is the fine-structure constant, with the inverse gravitational coupling $\hbar c / G m_p m_e$, up to a residual factor of $\approx 1.74$. We interpret this factor as a structural analogy with the cross-term that appears when an orthogonal quadratic form is replaced by the hexagonal (Eisenstein) norm $X^2 + XY + Y^2$. Applied naively as a free-particle dispersion relation, the cross-term produces a non-relativistic catastrophe: the group velocity acquires a constant $\sim c/2$ correction incompatible with cold dark matter. We resolve this by reinterpreting the Eisenstein geometry as governing the internal dynamics of a confined dark sector, analogous to QCD. The hexagonal norm describes mass generation inside a dark hadron, where constituent momenta are relativistic ($p \sim mc$), while the composite dark matter particle propagates with standard Lorentz-invariant kinematics. The $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3) provides the geometric link between a dark confining gauge sector and the GeV mass scale inferred from a cosmological geometric mean construction.

Notation and conventions

$\alpha = e^2 / 4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar c \approx 1/137.036$: fine-structure constant (Thomson limit)

$m_e, m_p, m_n$: electron, proton, neutron masses

$G, \hbar, c$: gravitational constant, reduced Planck constant, speed of light

1The standard relativistic quadratic form

The relativistic dispersion relation follows from the Minkowski norm of the four-momentum. For $P^\mu = (E/c,\, \vec{p}\,)$, the invariant mass condition is

$\displaystyle P_\mu P^\mu = m^2 c^2,$(1)

giving

$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4,$(2)

where $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ throughout. Setting $X = pc$ and $Y = mc^2$,

$\displaystyle E^2 = X^2 + Y^2.$(3)

This is the orthogonal quadratic form on two variables. The corresponding lattice is $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, the Gaussian integers. The orthogonality of $X$ and $Y$ reflects Lorentz invariance: momentum energy and rest-mass energy contribute independently.

2The Eisenstein hexagonal form

If the underlying geometry is hexagonal, with basis vectors at $60°$, the norm on $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ ($\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$) becomes

Eisenstein norm
$\displaystyle \|z\|^2 = X^2 + XY + Y^2.$(4)

Substituting $X = pc$ and $Y = mc^2$,

Eisenstein energy-momentum relation
$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + mc^2 \!\cdot\! pc + m^2 c^4.$(5)

The third term is the cross-term from the $60°$ lattice geometry. Because $p \geq 0$ and $m \geq 0$, the cross-term is non-negative. The physical domain of application is specified in §8.

3The completed square: hexagonal geometry made visible

The Eisenstein norm admits

$\displaystyle X^2 + XY + Y^2 = \left(X + \tfrac{1}{2}Y\right)^2 + \tfrac{3}{4}\,Y^2,$(6)

so

Completed-square form
$\displaystyle E^2 = \left(pc + \tfrac{1}{2}\,mc^2\right)^2 + \tfrac{3}{4}\,m^2 c^4.$(7)

The standard form is a circle in $(X,Y)$ space; the Eisenstein form is an ellipse with axes rotated by $30°$. The momentum axis picks up a rest-mass component ($\tfrac{1}{2}mc^2$); the mass axis is rescaled by $\sqrt{3}/2 = \cos 30°$.

At rest ($p = 0$): $E^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}m^2c^4 + \tfrac{3}{4}m^2c^4 = m^2c^4$. The total is preserved, but three quarters sits in the pure mass channel and one quarter in the shifted momentum channel. The rest mass has an intrinsic momentum-like component.

Geometric reading

In the Minkowski case, mass and momentum are perpendicular. In the Eisenstein case, they meet at $60°$. The completed square reveals the $30°$ rotation and $\cos 30°$ rescaling. The rest energy $mc^2$ is preserved, but its internal decomposition changes.

4The cross-term as geometric mean

The diagonal terms are $Y^2 = (mc^2)^2$ (rest-mass energy squared) and $X^2 = (pc)^2$ (momentum energy squared). Their geometric mean is

$\displaystyle \sqrt{Y^2 \cdot X^2} = XY = mc^2 \cdot pc.$(8)
Geometric mean structure

The Eisenstein cross-term is exactly the geometric mean of rest-mass energy squared and momentum energy squared. It couples the massive ($p = 0$) and massless ($m = 0$) limits. The Eisenstein geometry selects the coupling coefficient $\lambda = 1$.

5The Sirag coincidence and its discrepancy

The fine-structure constant is

$\displaystyle \alpha = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar c} \approx \frac{1}{137.036}.$(9)

Sirag [1] observed:

$\displaystyle N_\alpha = 2^{1/\alpha} \approx 1.79 \times 10^{41}, \qquad N_G = \frac{m_{\rm Pl}^2}{m_p m_e} \approx 3.11 \times 10^{41}.$(10)
Sirag factor
$\displaystyle \frac{N_G}{N_\alpha} \approx 1.74.$(11)

In logarithmic form: $\ln N_G = \ln N_\alpha + 0.554$.

6The discrepancy as structural analogue of the cross-term

The connection is a structural analogy. In the Eisenstein norm the cross-term increases the total by

$\displaystyle \frac{X^2 + XY + Y^2}{X^2 + Y^2} = 1 + \frac{XY}{X^2 + Y^2},$(12)

which lies in $(1, 1.5]$. The Sirag factor of 1.74 exceeds this range, so the identification is not exact at the level of the norm ratio. Both are $\mathcal{O}(1)$ corrections arising from departure from orthogonality.

Concretely: imposing $2^{1/\alpha} = m_{\rm Pl}^2 / (m_e m_X)$ gives $m_X \approx 1.74\, m_p \approx 1.6$ GeV$/c^2$. The question is what physics produces this effective mass.

7The non-relativistic catastrophe

If equation (5) is taken as a literal free-particle dispersion relation for dark matter, the group velocity (derived in Appendix B) is

$\displaystyle v_g = \frac{\partial E}{\partial p} = \frac{2pc^2 + mc^3}{2E} = \frac{pc^2}{E} + \frac{mc^3}{2E}.$(13)

The first term is the standard result. The second is new. In the non-relativistic limit ($p \ll mc$, $E \approx mc^2$),

$\displaystyle \Delta v \;\to\; \frac{c}{2}.$(14)
Non-relativistic catastrophe

A dark matter particle with any nonzero momentum would have a group velocity no less than $\sim c/2$. This is incompatible with cold dark matter, which requires $v \ll c$ for gravitational clustering. Structure formation, halo virialisation, and the Bullet Cluster dynamics all require non-relativistic dark matter kinematics.

7.1   The Bullet Cluster test

The sub-cluster velocity in 1E 0657-56 was $\sim$4700 km/s ($v/c \approx 0.016$). For a 2 GeV dark matter particle: $pc \approx 0.032$ GeV, $mc^2 = 2$ GeV, $E \approx 2.016$ GeV. The cross-term contributes $\sim$1.6% to $E^2$. But the group velocity correction $mc^3/2E \approx 0.496\,c$ dwarfs the actual velocity by a factor of thirty.

7.2   Galactic rotation curves

Flat rotation curves require virialised halos with $\rho \propto r^{-2}$. An enhanced group velocity shifts virial equilibrium: halos would be hotter, more extended, and less bound than observed.

7.3   Conclusion

Equation (5) cannot be the free-particle dispersion relation for dark matter. The cross-term is linear in $p$ and does not decouple at low momenta.

8Resolution: internal geometry of a confined dark sector

The resolution is to recognise where the Eisenstein geometry lives.

In QCD, the proton mass ($\sim$938 MeV) is overwhelmingly generated by confined quark-gluon dynamics, not by bare quark masses ($m_u + m_d \sim 10$ MeV). Inside the proton, constituent quarks are highly relativistic: momenta of order $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200$ MeV, comparable to their effective masses. This is the regime $p \sim mc$ where the cross-term is largest.

The composite proton, however, propagates with the standard dispersion relation $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m_p^2c^4$. Confinement separates internal from external kinematics.

Internal vs external kinematics

Internally: constituents of the dark hadron obey the Eisenstein relation $E^2 = X^2 + XY + Y^2$, where $X$ and $Y$ are constituent momentum and mass energies. The hexagonal geometry governs the mass-generating dynamics.

Externally: the composite dark hadron propagates with standard $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m_{\rm dm}^2c^4$. It is cold, collisionless, and non-relativistic in halos.

8.1   Where the hexagonal geometry lives

In QCD, the internal symmetry space of SU(3)$_c$ has the hexagonal $A_2$ root lattice. This geometry determines the hadron mass spectrum through confinement. Analogously, in a dark SU(3) sector, the Eisenstein norm on $A_2$ governs mass generation. The dark hadron mass is a dynamical consequence of the dark gauge geometry.

8.2   Why the cross-term matters for mass generation

In the orthogonal case, confined-state mass is built from $E^2 \sim \sum X_i^2 + \sum Y_i^2$. In the Eisenstein case, cross-terms $X_i Y_i$ contribute. Since these are the geometric means of rest-mass and momentum contributions, they interpolate between the two. The Sirag factor of 1.74 measures how much the hexagonal cross-terms contribute to the dark hadron mass, relative to an orthogonal geometry.

8.3   The QCD analogy

QCD parallel

QCD: SU(3)$_c$, root lattice $A_2$ (hexagonal), $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200$ MeV, proton $\sim 938$ MeV $\sim$ few $\times\Lambda_{\rm QCD}$. Internal quarks relativistic. External proton: standard dispersion.

Dark sector (proposed): SU(3)$_{\rm dark}$ (or similar), root lattice $A_2$, $\Lambda_{\rm dark} \sim \Lambda_{\rm QCD}$, dark hadron $\sim 2$ GeV $\sim$ few $\times\Lambda_{\rm dark}$. Internal dark quarks relativistic, governed by Eisenstein norm. External dark hadron: standard dispersion.

9Connection with the $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3)

The Cartan matrix of SU(3) is

$\displaystyle A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$(15)

The quadratic form on the root space is $\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{x} = a^2 - ab + b^2$. Under $b \to -b$ this becomes $a^2 + ab + b^2$, the Eisenstein norm. The lattice is unchanged: both forms represent the same $A_2$ hexagonal lattice up to basis choice.

Key observation

The Eisenstein norm is the natural quadratic form on the SU(3) root lattice. If a dark confining sector has SU(3)-like structure, the hexagonal geometry is inherited from the gauge symmetry. The cross-term is native to the root system of the confining group.

10Symmetry: isotropy, CPT, and the preferred frame

Under the reinterpretation of §8, the Eisenstein norm governs internal dynamics, not free-particle propagation. The following applies to the internal sector.

10.1   Spatial isotropy

The cross-term depends on $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ and $m$, both SO(3)-invariant. The internal dynamics preserve spatial isotropy.

10.2   CPT behaviour

Under C, P, T: $m$ is invariant under all three. $|\vec{p}\,|$ is invariant under C; invariant under P ($\vec{p} \to -\vec{p}$ but $|\vec{p}\,|$ unchanged); invariant under T ($\vec{p} \to -\vec{p}$ but $|\vec{p}\,|$ unchanged). The cross-term $mc^2 \cdot |\vec{p}\,|c$ is CPT-even.

On the CPT theorem

The Lüders-Pauli-Jost theorem derives CPT from Lorentz invariance + locality + unitarity. If internal dynamics break boost invariance, the theorem does not apply. CPT is imposed as a dark-sector symmetry assumption. The ansatz is CPT-even as shown above. This parallels the Boyle-Finn-Turok approach [5] where CPT is a cosmological boundary condition.

10.3   The preferred frame

Boost-violating internal dynamics define a preferred frame. The CMB rest frame is the natural choice. Since internal dynamics are confined at scales $\lesssim \Lambda_{\rm dark}^{-1}$, this does not affect macroscopic behaviour.

11The GeV mass scale from geometric mean construction

The Sirag factor defines $m_X \approx 1.74\, m_p \approx 1.6$ GeV$/c^2$. Decomposing via the mass-fraction weighted geometric mean,

$\displaystyle m_X = m_p^{\,1-f}\; m_{\rm dm}^{\,f}, \qquad f \approx 0.84,$(16)
Inferred dark matter mass
$\displaystyle m_{\rm dm} = m_p \left(\frac{m_X}{m_p}\right)^{1/f} \approx 2\;\text{GeV}/c^2.$(17)

Justification for using $m_p$ as the baryonic scale is in Appendix A.

12Observational consequences

12.1   Galactic rotation curves

The composite dark hadron has standard free-particle kinematics: cold, non-relativistic, collisionless. Rotation curve phenomenology is unchanged from CDM. However, internal dark-hadron structure could produce form-factor effects in self-interactions (analogous to finite-size effects in nucleon-nucleon scattering), potentially addressing the cusp-core problem at galactic centres.

12.2   The Bullet Cluster

The constraint $\sigma/m \lesssim 1$ cm$^2$/g applies to composite dark hadrons with standard external kinematics. The internal Eisenstein geometry is invisible at the Bullet Cluster collision scale ($E_{\rm cm} \sim$ keV per particle), far below $\Lambda_{\rm dark} \sim$ GeV.

12.3   Structure formation

A 2 GeV composite dark hadron with standard kinematics satisfies cold dark matter requirements: $m \gg$ keV and $v \ll c$ at matter-radiation equality.

12.4   Dark Cherenkov radiation and the dispersive dark vacuum

The Eisenstein dispersion relation can be read as propagation through an effective medium. Define an effective dark refractive index $n_{\rm dark}$ via the phase velocity,

$\displaystyle v_{\rm ph} = \frac{E}{p} = \frac{c\,\sqrt{X^2 + XY + Y^2}}{X/c}.$(18)

In the standard (orthogonal) case, $v_{\rm ph} = E/p = c\sqrt{1 + (mc/p)^2}$, which is always superluminal for massive particles. The Eisenstein cross-term modifies this: it changes the relationship between phase velocity and group velocity, making the effective dark vacuum dispersive.

In a dispersive medium, Cherenkov radiation occurs when a charged particle exceeds the local phase velocity of the radiation field. The dark analogue: if dark constituents inside a dark hadron exceed the phase velocity of some dark radiation field (dark gluons, for instance), they would emit dark Cherenkov radiation. This is formally identical to gluon bremsstrahlung in QCD, but with the modified dispersion relation changing the emission kinematics.

For the composite dark hadron, the external kinematics are standard and no vacuum Cherenkov effect occurs. This is consistent with the absence of vacuum Cherenkov constraints on dark matter from cosmic ray observations [4]. The Cherenkov phenomenology is confined to the interior of the dark hadron, where it contributes to the binding dynamics rather than producing observable radiation.

12.5   Gravitational wave signatures

If the dark sector undergoes a confining phase transition (analogous to the QCD transition at $T \sim 150$ MeV), this could produce a gravitational wave background. For $\Lambda_{\rm dark} \sim \Lambda_{\rm QCD}$, the signal would lie in the nanohertz band probed by pulsar timing arrays.

13Discussion

(i) The Sirag factor $N_G/N_\alpha \approx 1.74$ and the Eisenstein cross-term are both $\mathcal{O}(1)$ corrections from departure from orthogonality. The connection is a structural analogy.

(ii) The Eisenstein norm $X^2 + XY + Y^2$ has the cross-term $XY$ as geometric mean of $X^2$ and $Y^2$. The completed square reveals a $30°$ rotation and $\cos 30°$ rescaling.

(iii) As a literal free-particle dispersion, the Eisenstein norm produces a $c/2$ group-velocity floor, excluded by structure formation, rotation curves, and the Bullet Cluster.

(iv) The resolution: the Eisenstein geometry governs internal dynamics of a confined dark sector where constituent momenta are relativistic. The composite dark hadron has standard Lorentz-invariant external kinematics. This parallels QCD.

(v) The geometric mean construction yields $m_{\rm dm} \approx 2$ GeV, consistent with dark confinement at $\Lambda_{\rm dark} \sim \Lambda_{\rm QCD}$. The internal dynamics are isotropic and CPT-even, with CPT imposed as a symmetry assumption. The dispersive dark vacuum admits dark Cherenkov phenomenology confined to the dark hadron interior.

Summary

The Sirag factor is structurally analogous to the Eisenstein cross-term. The Eisenstein geometry is the root geometry of SU(3). The mass scale it produces is the confinement scale. The cross-term is the geometric mean of rest-mass and momentum contributions. The completed square reveals the $30°$ rotation and $\cos 30°$ rescaling. The Eisenstein norm governs internal mass-generating dynamics, not free-particle kinematics. The composite dark hadron propagates with standard Lorentz-invariant dispersion. These observations are not independent.

ACosmological abundance justification

The choice of $m_p m_e$ in $\alpha_g = Gm_pm_e/\hbar c$ is justified by cosmological abundances.

A.1   Baryon abundances

At $T_{\rm CMB} \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-4}$ eV, any baryon heavier than the nucleon is suppressed by $\exp(-\Delta mc^2/kT)$. For the $\Lambda$ ($\Delta mc^2 \approx 177$ MeV): $\exp(-7.7 \times 10^5) \approx 0$. Only nucleons survive. BBN gives $\sim$87.5% protons, $\sim$12.5% neutrons. The geometric mean nucleon mass satisfies $m_N/m_p \approx 1.00017$: negligible.

A.2   Charged lepton abundances

Muon ($\tau \approx 2.2\;\mu$s) and tau ($\tau \approx 2.9 \times 10^{-13}$ s) decay rapidly. Only electrons persist. Charge neutrality gives $n_e = n_p$. The equal-weight arithmetic and geometric means of $(m_e, m_\mu, m_\tau)$ are $\sim$628 MeV and $\sim$46 MeV: both wrong. The electron is selected by persistence.

A.3   Neutrino abundances

The cosmic neutrino background has $\sim$336 per cm$^3$ (all flavours) [3], outnumbering baryons by $\sim 10^9$. Neutrino masses ($\sim$10 to 50 meV) contribute negligibly to the gravitational coupling.

A.4   Number densities

Order of magnitude, from [3]

Baryons: $n_b \sim 2.5 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$ ($\sim$87.5% p, $\sim$12.5% n)

Electrons: $n_e \sim 2.2 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$

Neutrinos: $n_\nu \sim 336$ cm$^{-3}$

$N_{\rm baryons} \sim 10^{80}$, giving $\sqrt{N} \sim 10^{40}$: the Dirac coincidence.

BGroup velocity from a modified dispersion relation

B.1   Phase velocity and group velocity

For a wave with angular frequency $\omega$ and wavevector magnitude $k$, two velocities are defined:

$\displaystyle v_{\rm ph} = \frac{\omega}{k} \qquad \text{(phase velocity: speed of a single crest)}$(B.1)
$\displaystyle v_g = \frac{\partial \omega}{\partial k} \qquad \text{(group velocity: speed of the wave packet envelope)}$(B.2)

The group velocity is the speed at which energy and information propagate. When light enters a medium with refractive index $n(\omega)$, the phase velocity becomes $c/n$ and the group velocity becomes $c\,/\,(n + \omega\, dn/d\omega)$. These differ whenever the medium is dispersive ($dn/d\omega \neq 0$).

B.2   Application to massive particles

For a massive particle, the de Broglie relations give

$\displaystyle E = \hbar\omega, \qquad p = \hbar k.$(B.3)

Therefore $\omega = E/\hbar$ and $k = p/\hbar$. The group velocity becomes

$\displaystyle v_g = \frac{\partial \omega}{\partial k} = \frac{\partial(E/\hbar)}{\partial(p/\hbar)} = \frac{\partial E}{\partial p}.$(B.4)

This is the velocity at which the particle, treated as a quantum-mechanical wave packet, actually moves through space. For a free relativistic particle with $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m^2c^4$, differentiating gives $v_g = pc^2/E$, which in the non-relativistic limit reduces to $v = p/m$: the classical velocity.

The group velocity is therefore the physically relevant speed. It enters the virial theorem, the Boltzmann equation, structure formation calculations, and collision dynamics.

B.3   Derivation for the Eisenstein dispersion relation

We have $E^2 = p^2c^2 + mpc^3 + m^2c^4$, with $m$ constant and $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ the variable. To find $v_g = \partial E / \partial p$, differentiate both sides with respect to $p$.

Left side. By the chain rule,

$\displaystyle \frac{\partial}{\partial p}(E^2) = 2E \cdot \frac{\partial E}{\partial p}.$(B.5)

Combining with Right side,

$\displaystyle 2E \cdot \frac{\partial E}{\partial p} = 2pc^2 + mc^3.$(B.9)

Dividing both sides by $2E$,

Group velocity
$\displaystyle v_g = \frac{\partial E}{\partial p} = \frac{2pc^2 + mc^3}{2E} = \underbrace{\frac{pc^2}{E}}_{\text{standard}} + \underbrace{\frac{mc^3}{2E}}_{\text{cross-term correction}}.$(B.10)

B.4   The non-relativistic limit

For $p \ll mc$ (slow particle), $E \approx mc^2$. The standard term gives $pc^2/E \approx p/m = v$, the classical velocity. The cross-term correction gives

$\displaystyle \frac{mc^3}{2mc^2} = \frac{c}{2}.$(B.11)

This is constant, independent of $p$, and enormous. A particle with arbitrarily small momentum would still have group velocity $\gtrsim c/2$. This is the non-relativistic catastrophe of §7.

B.5   Why "group velocity" is the right quantity

For massive particles, the group velocity is the physical velocity: the speed at which mass, energy, and information travel through space. The phase velocity $v_{\rm ph} = E/p$ for a massive particle is always superluminal ($v_{\rm ph} = c^2/v_g > c$ in the standard case) and carries no energy. The group velocity is therefore the quantity that enters the virial theorem, the Boltzmann equation, structure formation, and collision dynamics such as the Bullet Cluster. The $c/2$ floor on $v_g$ is a physical prediction, not a mathematical artefact, which is why it excludes the naive free-particle interpretation and motivates the confined-sector reinterpretation of §8.
