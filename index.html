Eisenstein Geometry and the Dark Mass-Generating Mechanism: a Cross-Term Interpretation of the Sirag Factor

Lee McCulloch-James
Draft, 2026
Abstract

The Sirag large-number coincidence equates $2^{1/\alpha}$, where $\alpha \approx 1/137$ is the fine-structure constant, with the inverse gravitational coupling $\hbar c / G m_p m_e$, up to a residual factor of $\approx 1.74$. We interpret this factor as a structural analogy with the cross-term that appears when an orthogonal quadratic form is replaced by the hexagonal (Eisenstein) norm $X^2 + XY + Y^2$. Applied naively as a free-particle dispersion relation, the cross-term produces a non-relativistic catastrophe: the group velocity acquires a constant $\sim c/2$ correction that is incompatible with cold dark matter structure formation. We resolve this by reinterpreting the Eisenstein geometry as governing the internal dynamics of a confined dark sector, analogous to QCD, rather than the free-particle kinematics of composite dark matter. The hexagonal norm then describes the mass-generating mechanism inside a dark hadron, where constituent momenta are relativistic ($p \sim mc$), while the composite dark matter particle propagates with standard Lorentz-invariant kinematics. The connection to the $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3) links the hexagonal geometry to a dark confining gauge sector and the GeV mass scale inferred from a cosmological geometric mean construction.

1The standard relativistic quadratic form

The relativistic dispersion relation follows from the Minkowski norm of the four-momentum. For a particle with $P^\mu = (E/c,\, \vec{p}\,)$, the invariant mass condition is

$\displaystyle P_\mu P^\mu = m^2 c^2,$(1)

giving

$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4,$(2)

where $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ throughout. Setting $X = pc$ and $Y = mc^2$, both positive scalars with dimensions of energy,

$\displaystyle E^2 = X^2 + Y^2.$(3)

This is the Euclidean norm: the orthogonal quadratic form on two variables. The corresponding lattice is $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, the Gaussian integers. The orthogonality of $X$ and $Y$ reflects Lorentz invariance: momentum energy and rest-mass energy contribute independently.

2The Eisenstein hexagonal form

If the underlying geometry is hexagonal, with basis vectors at $60°$, the norm on $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ ($\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$) becomes

Eisenstein norm
$\displaystyle \|z\|^2 = X^2 + XY + Y^2.$(4)

Substituting $X = pc$ and $Y = mc^2$ gives

Eisenstein energy-momentum relation
$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + mc^2 \!\cdot\! pc + m^2 c^4.$(5)

The third term is the cross-term introduced by the $60°$ lattice geometry. Because $p = |\vec{p}\,| \geq 0$ and $m \geq 0$, the cross-term is non-negative and well-defined. For now we write equation (5) as an algebraic relation; its physical domain of application will be specified in §8.

3The completed square: hexagonal geometry made visible

The Eisenstein norm admits the completion of the square

$\displaystyle X^2 + XY + Y^2 = \left(X + \tfrac{1}{2}Y\right)^2 + \tfrac{3}{4}\,Y^2.$(6)

Substituting back,

Completed-square form
$\displaystyle E^2 = \left(pc + \tfrac{1}{2}\,mc^2\right)^2 + \tfrac{3}{4}\,m^2 c^4.$(7)

The standard orthogonal form $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$ describes a circle in $(X, Y)$ space. The Eisenstein form describes an ellipse with axes rotated by $30°$. The completed square exhibits this rotation: the momentum axis has been tilted to pick up a rest-mass component ($\tfrac{1}{2}mc^2$), while the mass axis has been rescaled by $\sqrt{3}/2 = \cos 30°$.

At rest ($p = 0$): $E^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}m^2c^4 + \tfrac{3}{4}m^2c^4 = m^2c^4$, so $E = mc^2$ is recovered. But the decomposition changes: three quarters sits in the pure mass channel and one quarter in the shifted momentum channel. The rest mass has an intrinsic momentum-like component.

Geometric reading

In the orthogonal (Minkowski) case, mass and momentum are perpendicular axes. In the Eisenstein case, they meet at $60°$. The completed square reveals the $30°$ rotation and the $\cos 30°$ rescaling that distinguish the hexagonal metric. The rest energy $mc^2$ is preserved, but its internal decomposition changes.

4The cross-term as geometric mean

The two diagonal terms are $Y^2 = (mc^2)^2$ and $X^2 = (pc)^2$: inertial rest-mass energy squared and radiation-like momentum energy squared. Their geometric mean is

$\displaystyle \sqrt{Y^2 \cdot X^2} = XY = mc^2 \cdot pc.$(8)
Geometric mean structure

The Eisenstein cross-term is exactly the geometric mean of the two quantities it interpolates: rest-mass energy squared and radiation momentum energy squared. It couples the massive ($p = 0$) and massless ($m = 0$) limits. In any quadratic form $X^2 + \lambda XY + Y^2$, the cross-term $XY$ is the geometric mean of $X^2$ and $Y^2$. The Eisenstein geometry selects $\lambda = 1$.

5The Sirag coincidence and its discrepancy

Let $\alpha$ denote the fine-structure constant,

$\displaystyle \alpha = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar c} \approx \frac{1}{137.036}.$(9)

Sirag [1] observed that two dimensionless quantities are numerically close:

$\displaystyle N_\alpha = 2^{1/\alpha} \approx 1.79 \times 10^{41}, \qquad N_G = \frac{m_{\rm Pl}^2}{m_p m_e} \approx 3.11 \times 10^{41}.$(10)
Sirag factor
$\displaystyle \frac{N_G}{N_\alpha} \approx 1.74.$(11)

Taking logarithms: $\ln N_G = \ln N_\alpha + \ln 1.74$, with $\ln 1.74 \approx 0.554$.

6The discrepancy as structural analogue of the cross-term

The connection between the Sirag factor and the Eisenstein cross-term is a structural analogy, not a derivation.

In the Eisenstein norm the cross-term increases the total by

$\displaystyle \frac{X^2 + XY + Y^2}{X^2 + Y^2} = 1 + \frac{XY}{X^2 + Y^2}.$(12)

For $X \neq Y$ this ratio lies strictly between 1 and 1.5. The Sirag factor of 1.74 exceeds this range, so the identification is not exact at the level of the norm ratio. The analogy operates at the level of structure: both the Sirag discrepancy and the Eisenstein cross-term are $\mathcal{O}(1)$ multiplicative corrections arising from departure from orthogonality.

Concretely: imposing $2^{1/\alpha} = m_{\rm Pl}^2 / (m_e m_X)$ gives $m_X \approx 1.74\, m_p \approx 1.6$ GeV$/c^2$. The question is what physics produces this effective mass.

7The non-relativistic catastrophe

Before proposing a physical interpretation, we must examine what happens if equation (5) is taken as a literal free-particle dispersion relation for dark matter.

The group velocity is

$\displaystyle v_g = \frac{\partial E}{\partial p} = \frac{2pc^2 + mc^3}{2E} = \frac{pc^2}{E} + \frac{mc^3}{2E}.$(13)

The first term is the standard result. The second is new: a velocity correction proportional to $mc^3 / 2E$ that is always positive. In the non-relativistic limit ($p \ll mc$), $E \approx mc^2$, and the correction approaches

$\displaystyle \Delta v \;\to\; \frac{c}{2}.$(14)
Non-relativistic catastrophe

A dark matter particle with any nonzero momentum, no matter how small, would have a group velocity no less than $\sim c/2$. This is incompatible with cold dark matter, which requires $v \ll c$ for gravitational clustering. Structure formation, galactic halo virialisation, and the observed dynamics of systems like the Bullet Cluster all require non-relativistic dark matter kinematics.

7.1   The Bullet Cluster test

In the Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56), the sub-cluster velocity relative to the main cluster was approximately 4700 km/s, so $v/c \approx 0.016$. For a 2 GeV dark matter particle at this velocity: $pc \approx 0.032$ GeV, $mc^2 = 2$ GeV, giving $E \approx 2.016$ GeV. The cross-term contributes about 1.6% to $E^2$. But the group velocity correction $mc^3/2E \approx 0.496\,c$ dwarfs the actual velocity by a factor of thirty. The dark matter halos could not have passed through each other as observed; they would behave as a relativistic fluid.

7.2   Galactic rotation curves

Flat rotation curves require dark matter to form extended, virialised halos with density profiles $\rho \propto r^{-2}$ at intermediate radii. Virial equilibrium relates kinetic and potential energies. If the kinetic energy of dark matter particles at a given momentum is enhanced by the cross-term, the virial equilibrium shifts: halos would be hotter, more extended, and less gravitationally bound than observed.

7.3   Conclusion

Equation (5) cannot be the free-particle dispersion relation for dark matter. The cross-term does not decouple at low momenta because it is linear in $p$. Any literal application to non-relativistic dark matter kinematics is excluded by observation.

8Resolution: internal geometry of a confined dark sector

The resolution comes from recognising where the Eisenstein geometry lives.

In QCD, the proton mass ($\sim 938$ MeV) is overwhelmingly generated by the dynamics of confined quarks and gluons, not by the bare quark masses ($m_u + m_d \sim 10$ MeV). Inside the proton, the constituent quarks are highly relativistic: their momenta are of order $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200$ MeV, comparable to their effective masses. The internal dynamics are in the regime $p \sim mc$, precisely where the cross-term is largest.

The composite proton, however, propagates as a free particle with the standard Lorentz-invariant dispersion relation $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m_p^2 c^4$. The internal structure is invisible to the external kinematics. Confinement ensures this separation.

We propose that the same separation applies to a dark confined sector:

Internal vs external kinematics

Internally: the constituents of the dark hadron (dark quarks, dark gluons) obey the Eisenstein energy-momentum relation $E^2 = X^2 + XY + Y^2$, where $X$ and $Y$ are the constituent momentum energy and constituent mass energy. The hexagonal geometry governs the mass-generating dynamics of the confined sector.

Externally: the composite dark hadron propagates with the standard Lorentz-invariant dispersion relation $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m_{\rm dm}^2 c^4$. It is cold, collisionless, and non-relativistic in galactic halos, consistent with all observational constraints.

The cross-term is active inside the dark hadron, at scales $\lesssim \Lambda_{\rm dark}^{-1}$, where the constituent momenta are relativistic. It is invisible at the macroscopic scales of halos, clusters, and structure formation, where only the composite mass $m_{\rm dm}$ enters.

8.1   Where the hexagonal geometry lives

In QCD, the internal symmetry space of SU(3)$_c$ is hexagonal (the $A_2$ root lattice). This internal geometry determines the mass spectrum of hadrons through confinement dynamics. The proton mass is not the sum of quark masses; it is a dynamical consequence of the gauge geometry.

Analogously, in a dark SU(3) sector, the Eisenstein norm on the $A_2$ root lattice would govern the internal mass-generating dynamics. The composite dark hadron mass $m_{\rm dm}$ is a dynamical consequence of the dark gauge geometry, not the sum of dark constituent masses.

8.2   Why the cross-term matters for mass generation

In the orthogonal (Gaussian) case, the mass of a confined state is built from the sum of squared constituent energies: $E^2 \sim \sum (p_i c)^2 + \sum (m_i c^2)^2$. In the hexagonal (Eisenstein) case, the cross-terms $m_i c^2 \cdot p_i c$ contribute as well. Since these are the geometric means of the rest-mass and momentum contributions, they interpolate between the two. The result is a modified relationship between the constituent dynamics and the composite mass.

The Sirag factor of 1.74 then measures the degree to which the hexagonal cross-terms contribute to the dark hadron mass, relative to what an orthogonal geometry would produce.

8.3   The QCD analogy made explicit

QCD parallel

QCD: gauge group SU(3)$_c$, root lattice $A_2$ (hexagonal), confinement scale $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200$ MeV, composite proton mass $\sim 938$ MeV $\sim$ a few $\times \Lambda_{\rm QCD}$. Internal quarks highly relativistic. External proton obeys standard dispersion relation.

Dark sector (proposed): gauge group SU(3)$_{\rm dark}$ (or similar), root lattice $A_2$ (hexagonal), confinement scale $\Lambda_{\rm dark} \sim \Lambda_{\rm QCD}$, composite dark hadron mass $\sim 2$ GeV $\sim$ a few $\times \Lambda_{\rm dark}$. Internal dark quarks highly relativistic, governed by Eisenstein norm. External dark hadron obeys standard dispersion relation.

9Connection with the $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3)

The Cartan matrix of SU(3) is

$\displaystyle A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$(15)

The corresponding quadratic form on the root space is $\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{x}^T A\,\mathbf{x} = a^2 - ab + b^2$. Under the reflection $b \to -b$ this becomes $a^2 + ab + b^2$, the Eisenstein norm. The lattice generated is unchanged: both forms represent the same $A_2$ hexagonal lattice up to basis choice.

Key observation

The Eisenstein norm is the natural quadratic form on the root lattice of SU(3). If a dark confining gauge sector has SU(3)-like structure, the hexagonal geometry is inherited from the gauge symmetry. The cross-term is not imposed ad hoc; it is the kinematic form native to the root system of the confining group.

10Symmetry: isotropy, CPT, and the preferred frame

Under the reinterpretation of §8, the Eisenstein norm governs the internal dynamics of dark confinement, not the free-particle propagation of composite dark matter. The external kinematics are Lorentz-invariant. The following symmetry discussion therefore applies to the internal mass-generating sector.

10.1   Spatial isotropy

The cross-term depends only on $p = |\vec{p}\,|$ and $m$, both invariant under spatial rotations. The internal dynamics therefore preserve SO(3).

10.2   CPT behaviour

Under C, P, T individually: $m$ is invariant under all three; $|\vec{p}\,|$ is invariant under C, P (parity flips $\vec{p}$ but not $|\vec{p}\,|$), and T (time reversal flips $\vec{p}$ but not $|\vec{p}\,|$). The cross-term $mc^2 \cdot |\vec{p}\,|c$ is therefore invariant under each of C, P, T separately, and under CPT.

On the CPT theorem

The standard CPT theorem (Lüders, Pauli, Jost) derives CPT invariance from Lorentz invariance together with locality and unitarity. If the internal dynamics break boost invariance, the theorem does not apply. CPT invariance of the dark sector is therefore not guaranteed but can be imposed as a symmetry assumption. The ansatz is CPT-even at the level of the one-particle relation, as shown above.

This is consistent with the Boyle-Finn-Turok approach [5], where CPT is a cosmological boundary condition rather than a consequence of local field theory.

10.3   The preferred frame

A boost-violating internal dynamics defines a preferred frame. The natural choice is the CMB rest frame. Since the internal dynamics are confined at scales $\lesssim \Lambda_{\rm dark}^{-1}$, the preferred-frame aspect does not affect the macroscopic behaviour of dark matter, which propagates with standard Lorentz-invariant kinematics.

11The GeV mass scale from geometric mean construction

The Sirag factor of 1.74 defines an effective mass $m_X \approx 1.74\, m_p \approx 1.6$ GeV$/c^2$ via $2^{1/\alpha} = m_{\rm Pl}^2 / (m_e m_X)$. Decomposing into baryonic and dark contributions by the mass-fraction weighted geometric mean

$\displaystyle m_X = m_p^{\,1-f}\; m_{\rm dm}^{\,f}, \qquad f \approx 0.84,$(16)

and solving,

Inferred dark matter mass
$\displaystyle m_{\rm dm} = m_p \left(\frac{m_X}{m_p}\right)^{1/f} \approx 2\;\text{GeV}/c^2.$(17)

This is the GeV scale generated by QCD confinement. Under the present interpretation, it arises because the Eisenstein cross-term in the internal mass-generating dynamics and the confining gauge geometry share a common origin in the $A_2$ root lattice.

The justification for using $m_p$ as the baryonic mass scale is given in Appendix A.

12Observational consequences

12.1   Galactic rotation curves

Under the reinterpretation of §8, the composite dark matter particle has standard free-particle kinematics. It is cold, non-relativistic, and collisionless at halo scales. Rotation curve phenomenology is therefore unchanged from standard CDM.

However, the internal structure of the dark hadron could in principle produce form-factor effects in dark matter self-interactions, analogous to the finite-size effects that appear in nucleon-nucleon scattering. These would modify the self-interaction cross-section at momentum transfers comparable to $\Lambda_{\rm dark}$, potentially addressing the cusp-core problem at galactic centres without affecting large-scale structure.

12.2   The Bullet Cluster

The Bullet Cluster constrains the dark matter self-interaction cross-section to $\sigma/m \lesssim 1$ cm$^2$/g. This constraint applies to the composite dark hadron, whose external kinematics are standard. The internal Eisenstein geometry is invisible at the collision energy scale of the Bullet Cluster ($E_{\rm cm}$ per particle $\sim$ keV), which is far below the dark confinement scale ($\sim$ GeV). The constraint is satisfied.

12.3   Structure formation

Cold dark matter structure formation requires $m_{\rm dm} \gtrsim$ keV and $v \ll c$ at matter-radiation equality. A 2 GeV composite dark hadron with standard free-particle kinematics satisfies both requirements. The Eisenstein internal dynamics produce the mass but do not modify the clustering behaviour.

12.4   Potential signatures

If the dark sector undergoes a confining phase transition in the early universe (analogous to the QCD phase transition at $T \sim 150$ MeV), this could produce a gravitational wave background at frequencies determined by $\Lambda_{\rm dark}$. For $\Lambda_{\rm dark} \sim \Lambda_{\rm QCD}$, the signal would lie in the nanohertz band probed by pulsar timing arrays.

13Discussion

(i) The Sirag factor $N_G / N_\alpha \approx 1.74$ is an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ multiplicative correction to a large-number coincidence. The Eisenstein cross-term is an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ multiplicative correction to a quadratic norm. We propose these share a common geometric origin, as a structural analogy.

(ii) The hexagonal quadratic form $X^2 + XY + Y^2$ is the Eisenstein norm on the $A_2$ root lattice. The cross-term $XY$ is the geometric mean of rest-mass energy squared and momentum energy squared. The completed-square form reveals the $30°$ rotation and $\cos 30°$ rescaling of the hexagonal metric.

(iii) Taken as a literal free-particle dispersion relation, the Eisenstein norm produces a non-relativistic catastrophe: a constant group-velocity correction of $\sim c/2$ that is excluded by structure formation, rotation curve dynamics, and Bullet Cluster observations.

(iv) The resolution is to locate the Eisenstein geometry inside a confined dark sector: it governs the internal dynamics of dark hadron mass generation, where constituent momenta are relativistic ($p \sim mc$), while the composite dark hadron propagates with standard Lorentz-invariant kinematics. This parallels QCD, where the hexagonal $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3)$_c$ governs internal dynamics but the proton has standard external kinematics.

(v) The geometric mean construction yields $m_{\rm dm} \approx 2$ GeV, consistent with a dark confinement scale comparable to $\Lambda_{\rm QCD}$. The internal Eisenstein dynamics are isotropic and CPT-even. CPT is imposed as a dark-sector symmetry, not derived from the Lüders-Pauli-Jost theorem, consistent with the Boyle-Finn-Turok approach.

Summary

The Sirag factor is structurally analogous to the Eisenstein cross-term. The Eisenstein geometry is the root geometry of SU(3). The mass scale it produces is the confinement scale. The cross-term is the geometric mean of the rest-mass and momentum contributions. The completed-square form reveals the $30°$ rotation and $\cos 30°$ rescaling of the hexagonal metric. The Eisenstein norm governs the internal mass-generating dynamics of a confined dark sector, not the free-particle kinematics. The composite dark hadron has standard Lorentz-invariant propagation. These observations are not independent.

ACosmological abundance justification

The choice of $m_p m_e$ as the mass product in $\alpha_g = G m_p m_e / \hbar c$ requires justification. Cosmological abundances select specific species overwhelmingly.

A.1   Baryon abundances

At $T_{\rm CMB} \approx 2.7$ K $\approx 2.3 \times 10^{-4}$ eV, any baryon heavier than the nucleon is suppressed by $\exp(-\Delta mc^2 / kT)$. For the $\Lambda$ baryon ($\Delta mc^2 \approx 177$ MeV):

$\displaystyle \exp\!\left(-\frac{177\;\text{MeV}}{2.3\times10^{-4}\;\text{eV}}\right) \approx \exp(-7.7\times10^{5}) \approx 0.$(A.1)

Only nucleons survive. Primordial helium synthesis gives $\sim$87.5% protons, $\sim$12.5% neutrons. The geometric mean nucleon mass $m_N = m_p^{0.875} m_n^{0.125}$ satisfies $m_N/m_p \approx 1.00017$: a correction at $\mathcal{O}(10^{-4})$, negligible here.

A.2   Charged lepton abundances

The muon ($\tau_\mu \approx 2.2\;\mu$s) and tau ($\tau_\tau \approx 2.9 \times 10^{-13}$ s) decay rapidly. Only electrons persist. Charge neutrality gives $n_e = n_p$. The arithmetic and geometric means of $(m_e, m_\mu, m_\tau)$ with equal weights are $\sim$628 MeV and $\sim$46 MeV, both far from the correct scale. The electron is selected by persistence, not averaging.

A.3   Neutrino abundances

The cosmic neutrino background contains $\sim$112 per cm$^3$ per flavour ($\sim$336 total) [3], outnumbering baryons by $\sim 10^9$. But neutrino masses ($\sim$10 to 50 meV) contribute negligibly to any gravitational coupling compared to the nucleon-electron product.

A.4   Number densities

Present-day number densities (order of magnitude, from [3])

Baryons: $n_b \sim 2.5 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$ ($\sim$87.5% p, $\sim$12.5% n)

Electrons: $n_e \sim 2.2 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$

Neutrinos: $n_\nu \sim 336$ cm$^{-3}$ (all flavours)

$N_{\rm baryons} \sim 10^{80}$ in the observable universe, giving $\sqrt{N} \sim 10^{40}$: the Dirac coincidence.
