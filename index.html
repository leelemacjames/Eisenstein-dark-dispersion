Eisenstein Geometry and the Dark Dispersion Relation: a Cross-Term Interpretation of the Sirag Factor

Draft, 2025
Abstract

The Sirag large-number coincidence equates $2^{1/\alpha}$ with the inverse gravitational coupling $\hbar c / G m_p m_e$, up to a residual factor of $\approx 1.74$. Rather than absorbing this discrepancy into a varying gravitational constant, we interpret it as the signature of a hexagonal (Eisenstein) geometry in the energy-momentum relation. The standard Minkowski dispersion relation $E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4$ corresponds to an orthogonal quadratic form. Replacing this with the Eisenstein norm $a^2 + ab + b^2$ introduces a cross-term $mpc^3$ that modifies the dispersion relation for dark matter specifically. The cross-term is the geometric mean of the two terms it interpolates: inertial rest-mass energy squared and photon-like momentum energy squared. The connection to the $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3) suggests a link between the hexagonal geometry of a dark confining gauge sector and the GeV mass scale inferred from the Sirag relation.

1The standard relativistic quadratic form

The relativistic dispersion relation

$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4$ (1)

corresponds to an orthogonal quadratic form. Setting $a = pc$ and $b = mc^2$, we have

$\displaystyle E^2 = a^2 + b^2.$ (2)

This is the norm on $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, the Gaussian integers. The underlying geometry is square: basis vectors at $90°$. The energy-momentum relation inherits Lorentz invariance from the rotational symmetry of the Euclidean plane under Wick rotation.

2The Eisenstein hexagonal form

If the underlying geometry is hexagonal, with basis vectors at $60°$, the norm becomes the Eisenstein form

Eisenstein norm
$\displaystyle \|z\|^2 = a^2 + ab + b^2.$ (3)

This is the norm on $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$, the Eisenstein integers, where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ is a primitive cube root of unity. Substituting the relativistic identifications $a = pc$, $b = mc^2$ gives

Modified dispersion relation
$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4 + mpc^3.$ (4)

The third term is the cross-term introduced by the $60°$ lattice geometry. It couples momentum and rest mass in a way that has no analogue in the standard Minkowski framework.

3The cross-term as geometric mean

The cross-term $mpc^3$ admits a simple interpretation. The two "diagonal" terms in the dispersion relation are

$\displaystyle (mc^2)^2 \quad \text{and} \quad (pc)^2.$ (5)

The first is the square of the inertial rest-mass energy. The second is the square of the photon-like momentum energy. Their geometric mean is

$\displaystyle \sqrt{(mc^2)^2 \cdot (pc)^2} \;=\; mc^2 \cdot pc \;=\; mpc^3.$ (6)
Geometric mean structure

The Eisenstein cross-term is exactly the geometric mean of the two quantities it interpolates: the rest-mass energy squared and the radiation momentum energy squared. It sits between the massive ($p = 0$) and massless ($m = 0$) limits, coupling the two regimes.

This is not accidental. In any quadratic form $a^2 + \lambda ab + b^2$, the cross-term $ab$ is the geometric mean of $a^2$ and $b^2$. The Eisenstein geometry selects $\lambda = 1$.

4The Sirag discrepancy in logarithmic space

Recall the two large dimensionless numbers

$\displaystyle N_\alpha = 2^{1/\alpha} \approx 1.79 \times 10^{41}, \qquad N_G = \frac{\hbar c}{G\, m_p\, m_e} \approx 3.11 \times 10^{41}.$ (7)

Their ratio is $N_G / N_\alpha \approx 1.74$. Taking logarithms

$\displaystyle \ln N_G = \ln N_\alpha + \ln 1.74.$ (8)

Numerically

$\displaystyle \ln 1.74 \approx 0.554.$ (9)

The Sirag discrepancy corresponds to an additive contribution in the logarithmic norm of magnitude $\approx 0.554$.

5Interpreting the extra factor as the cross-term

In a quadratic norm $L^2 = a^2 + b^2 + ab$, the cross-term $ab$ represents the departure from orthogonality. Relative to the orthogonal norm $a^2 + b^2$, the fractional correction is

$\displaystyle \frac{ab}{a^2 + b^2}.$ (10)

When $a$ and $b$ differ by an order of magnitude (as the logarithmic components of the large-number relation do), this correction is naturally of order unity. The numerical value

Cross-term identification
$\displaystyle e^{0.554} \approx 1.74$ (11)

is consistent with interpreting the Sirag factor as the Eisenstein cross-term.

6The dark dispersion relation

We propose that the modified dispersion relation

Dark sector dispersion
$\displaystyle E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4 + mpc^3$ (12)

applies specifically to dark matter, and not to baryonic matter.

The cross-term couples rest mass and spatial momentum. At rest ($p = 0$) the relation reduces to $E = mc^2$. For massless particles ($m = 0$) it reduces to $E = pc$. The modification appears only when both mass and momentum are nonzero. It is therefore invisible to photon propagation and to rest-mass measurements.

For baryonic matter, the orthogonal dispersion relation $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m^2c^4$ has been tested to extraordinary precision across the full range of particle physics experiments. Any cross-term for baryonic matter is excluded. The claim is therefore that the Eisenstein cross-term is a property of the dark sector specifically: it modifies the kinematics of massive dark particles in motion, while leaving baryonic kinematics and photon propagation untouched.

The cross-term, as shown in §3, is the geometric mean of the rest-mass energy squared and the momentum energy squared. It interpolates between the two regimes that baryonic physics treats as orthogonal. For dark matter, these regimes are not orthogonal but coupled at $60°$.

7Connection with the $A_2$ root lattice of SU(3)

The root lattice of the colour gauge group SU(3) is the hexagonal $A_2$ lattice. Its quadratic form is exactly

$\displaystyle a^2 + ab + b^2.$ (13)

This is not a coincidence imposed from outside. The Cartan matrix of SU(3) is

$\displaystyle A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix},$ (14)

and the corresponding quadratic form on the root space is $\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A\, \mathbf{x} = a^2 - ab + b^2$, or equivalently (under the substitution $b \to -b$) the Eisenstein norm $a^2 + ab + b^2$. The two forms generate the same lattice.

QCD, with gauge group SU(3)$_c$, generates the dominant contribution to the proton mass via confinement at scale $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200$ MeV. A dark confining gauge sector with SU(3)-like structure would naturally inherit the hexagonal geometry of the $A_2$ root lattice.

Key observation

The Eisenstein cross-term in the dispersion relation and the hexagonal root lattice of the confining gauge group share the same mathematical structure. The cross-term is not imposed ad hoc. It is the natural kinematic consequence of a gauge symmetry whose root system is hexagonal.

8On Poincaré invariance

The cross-term $mpc^3$ is not a Lorentz scalar. Under a boost, $m$ is invariant and $p$ transforms, so the product $mp$ is not boost-invariant. The modified dispersion relation therefore breaks Poincaré symmetry.

This is a strong claim. Poincaré invariance sits at the foundation of relativistic quantum field theory. The CPT theorem depends on it. No violation has been observed in any sector, baryonic or otherwise.

On the status of Poincaré invariance in the dark sector

We do not argue that Poincaré invariance is wrong. We note that it has been tested exclusively with electromagnetically coupled matter. The CPT theorem applies to quantum fields on a Poincaré-invariant background. Whether dark matter is described by such fields is an assumption, not a measurement.

The cross-term provides a specific, falsifiable form for a departure that would be invisible to all existing tests. It vanishes for massless particles. It vanishes at rest. It modifies only the kinematics of massive particles in motion within a sector that interacts with baryonic matter only gravitationally. Every precision test of Lorentz and CPT symmetry to date has been conducted with photons, electrons, protons, neutrons, or neutrinos. None has probed the dispersion relation of a particle species that does not couple to the electromagnetic field.

Lorentz-violating dispersion relations have been studied extensively in quantum gravity phenomenology, typically taking the form $E^2 = p^2c^2 + m^2c^4 + \eta\, p^n / M_{\rm Pl}^{n-2}$ for integer $n$. The Eisenstein form corresponds to $n = 1$ with the Planck suppression replaced by $mc^2$, giving a much larger effect. But the effect is confined to a sector that interacts with baryonic matter only gravitationally, placing it beyond the reach of existing Lorentz-violation searches.

9The GeV mass scale from geometric mean construction

As developed in the companion paper, the Sirag factor of 1.74 can be absorbed by defining an effective gravitating mass scale $m_X \approx 1.74\, m_p \approx 1.6$ GeV$/c^2$. Decomposing this into baryonic and dark contributions via the mass-fraction weighted geometric mean

$\displaystyle m_X = m_p^{\,1-f}\; m_{\rm dm}^{\,f}, \qquad f = \frac{\Omega_c}{\Omega_c + \Omega_b} \approx 0.84,$ (15)

yields

Dark matter mass from Eisenstein geometry
$\displaystyle m_{\rm dm} \approx 2\;\text{GeV}/c^2.$ (16)

This is the same GeV scale generated by QCD confinement. Under the present interpretation, it arises because the hexagonal cross-term and the confining gauge dynamics share a common geometric origin in the $A_2$ root lattice.

The justification for using $m_p$ (rather than an average over all baryon and lepton species) as the baryonic mass scale is given in Appendix A.

10Discussion

The chain of reasoning is:

(i) The Sirag factor $N_G / N_\alpha \approx 1.74$ is not noise. It is the exponential of the cross-term contribution ($e^{0.554}$) that arises when the quadratic form underlying the dispersion relation is hexagonal rather than orthogonal.

(ii) The hexagonal quadratic form $a^2 + ab + b^2$ is the Eisenstein norm, native to the $A_2$ root lattice. The cross-term $ab$ is the geometric mean of the two diagonal terms: rest-mass energy squared and momentum energy squared.

(iii) The $A_2$ lattice is the root lattice of SU(3). QCD, with gauge group SU(3)$_c$, generates the proton mass at the GeV scale via confinement. A dark confining sector with SU(3)-like structure would produce dark matter masses at a comparable scale.

(iv) The geometric mean construction, using the cosmological dark fraction $f \approx 0.84$, yields $m_{\rm dm} \approx 2$ GeV, consistent with a dark confinement scale comparable to $\Lambda_{\rm QCD}$.

(v) The modified dispersion relation breaks Poincaré invariance. This is a strong claim, mitigated by the fact that the cross-term vanishes at rest and for massless particles, and that no test of Poincaré symmetry has probed the dark sector directly.

Summary

The Sirag factor is the Eisenstein cross-term. The Eisenstein geometry is the root geometry of SU(3). The mass scale it produces is the confinement scale. The cross-term is the geometric mean of the rest-mass and momentum contributions to the dispersion relation. These four statements are not independent.

ACosmological abundance justification

The choice of $m_p m_e$ as the mass product in the gravitational coupling $\alpha_g = G m_p m_e / \hbar c$ requires justification. One might ask: why not average over all baryon and lepton species? The answer is that cosmological abundances select specific species overwhelmingly.

A.1   Baryon abundances

At the current CMB temperature $T_{\rm CMB} \approx 2.7$ K $\approx 2.3 \times 10^{-4}$ eV, any baryon heavier than the nucleon is suppressed by the Boltzmann factor $\exp(-\Delta mc^2 / kT)$. For the lightest strange baryon ($\Lambda$, mass gap $\Delta mc^2 \approx 177$ MeV above the nucleon):

$\displaystyle \exp\!\left(-\frac{177\;\text{MeV}}{2.3\times10^{-4}\;\text{eV}}\right) \approx \exp(-7.7\times10^{5}) \approx 0.$ (A.1)

The cosmic baryon population consists entirely of nucleons. Primordial helium synthesis fixes the mixture at approximately 87.5% protons and 12.5% neutrons ($n_p : n_n \approx 7 : 1$).

The number-weighted geometric mean nucleon mass is

$\displaystyle m_N = m_p^{\,0.875}\; m_n^{\,0.125}.$ (A.2)

Since $m_n / m_p \approx 1.00138$, this gives $m_N / m_p \approx 1.00017$, a correction at the level of $\mathcal{O}(10^{-4})$, which is negligible for the large-number relations.

A.2   Charged lepton abundances

The heavier charged leptons decay rapidly: $\tau_\mu \approx 2.2\;\mu$s, $\tau_\tau \approx 2.9 \times 10^{-13}$ s. Only electrons survive on cosmological timescales. Charge neutrality requires $n_e = n_p$.

The arithmetic and geometric means of the three charged lepton masses are:

$\displaystyle \langle m_\ell \rangle_{\rm arith} \approx 628\;\text{MeV} \approx 1228\; m_e, \qquad \langle m_\ell \rangle_{\rm geom} \approx 46\;\text{MeV} \approx 90\; m_e.$ (A.3)

Both are dominated by the tau mass and are far too heavy to appear in the gravitational coupling at the correct order of magnitude. The electron is not an "average lepton" but the only cosmologically persistent one.

A.3   Neutrino abundances

The cosmic neutrino background contains $\sim 112$ neutrinos per cm$^3$ per flavour ($\nu + \bar{\nu}$), giving $\sim 336$ total. These outnumber baryons by a factor of $\sim 10^9$. However, neutrino masses are of order $\sim 10$–$50$ meV, so the neutrino contribution to the gravitational coupling is negligible compared to the nucleon-electron product.

Any number-weighted lepton average is dominated by neutrino masses, pushing $1/\gamma$ to $\sim 10^{49}$, far from the Sirag target of $\sim 10^{41}$. Any mass-weighted average is dominated by the tau, giving $\sim 10^{43}$. Neither matches. The $m_p m_e$ product is selected by cosmological abundance, not by convention.

A.4   Summary of cosmological number densities

Present-day number densities

Baryons: $n_b \approx 2.5 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$ (of which 87.5% protons, 12.5% neutrons)
Electrons: $n_e \approx 2.2 \times 10^{-7}$ cm$^{-3}$ (charge neutrality with protons)
Neutrinos: $n_\nu \approx 336$ cm$^{-3}$ (all flavours, $\nu + \bar{\nu}$)
Neutrino/baryon ratio: $\sim 1.3 \times 10^{9}$

The number of baryons in the observable universe is $N_{\rm baryons} \approx 10^{80}$, giving $\sqrt{N} \approx 10^{40}$, which is the original Dirac large-number coincidence. Including dark matter ($\Omega_m \approx 0.315$) pushes $N_{\rm total} \approx 6.4 \times 10^{80}$ in proton-mass equivalents, giving $\sqrt{N} \approx 2.5 \times 10^{40}$. The traditional $10^{40}$ holds well for baryonic matter alone.
